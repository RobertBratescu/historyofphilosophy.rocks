Parmenides of Elea: Logic Demands a Changeless Universe

Parmenides of Elea: Logic Demands a Changeless Universe

Parmenides (480 BC) - Eleiaticism / Eleiatic philosophy

The first to support convictions with reasoned argument

Basic principle: What is, is, and what is not, is not, and what is not can neither be, nor be thought about

Systematic deductions

  1. The universe must be uncreated.
  2. The universe must be indestructable; it could never go out of existence. The universe is therefore eternal.
  3. Can there be such a thing as a vacuum? Absolutely not. The universe is a plenum (full).
  4. Change is impossible - change of any kind

"Father of Logic"

A seed growing into a flower. The seed is what is, the flower is what is not. The seed disappears and the flower appears. Change is a double violation of his principle: every change is a simultaneous passage from what is to what is not, ant what is not to what is. But there is no "what is not".

Agreement with Heraclitus: Change implies a contradiction - a violation of logic

Parmenides went on to deny multiplicity. It is an illusion.

Epistemologically: Rationalism

Parmenides' follower: Zeno (490 - 430 BC)

Paradoxes:

  • Motion is impossible
    It's impossible to cross a room. You have to cross half of it, and half of that, etc. You can subdivide infinitely.
    Applies to talking.
  • Multiplicity is impossible
    Multiplicity is the view that the world consists of a number of things. The world can't be a whole composed of parts.
    • If the world is a whole with parts, what size would the universe be and how many parts does it have? Infinite number of parts because every magnitude is theoretically divisible without limit and if there's an infinite number of parts we must have an infinitely big universe
    • What will be the size of the ultimate parts of the universe? There has to be such ultimate indivisible parts. But any magnitude is divisible. So the ultimate parts must be zero in size

Argument 1: The Argument from the Differences between Universals and Particulars

  1. There is one universal per category, but many particulars
  2. Particulars change, universals go on unchanging
  3. Particulars are primarily material; universals are abstract, nonphysical
  4. Particulars are known by means of the senses; universals are known by reason, intelect

Argument 2: The Argument of Perfection

Where do we get our concepts and standards of perfection

There is no such thing as a perfection in this world

  1. Things in this world change. If a thing changes, it couldn't be perfect, it would simply stand still, it would lack nothing
  2. Even in mathematics, the Platonists say, you'll never find perfection (perfect triangle, perfect line).
  3. We criticize things as imperfect; this presupposes that we know what perfection would consist of - and we couldn't have got it from this world

When we think of a universal, we usually have a perfect, unblemished representative in mind. The world of perfection is precisely the world of universals.

  1. Innate ideas - i.e., knowledge possessd in us at birth.
  2. The soul must be independent of the body - the groundwork to establish the immortality of the soul

Argument 3: The Argument from the Order of Knowledge

Which, in logic, would you have to learn first: universals or particulars?

Plato says it's impossible to know particulars. It is impossible to collect particular instances of justice and then abstract from them the concept of "justice". You must know beforehand what justice looks like.

There must be a realm of universals independent of this world which we knew before this life. We were born with all this knowledge, it's unconscious and we have to unearth it

Argument 4: The Argument from the Possibility of Knowledge

    You can't know a world of flux, but this world is a world of flux. If reality is to be knowable, it must be immutable - that means another world.
  1. If a world is changing, it's contradictory, and you can't know the contradictory. If a world is to be knowable, it must be made of consistent entities, entities which don't change.
  2. Knowledge acquired by the senses is invalid. Knowledges of reality must be acwuired by non-sensory means; and thus it must be of a non-material object.
  3. The crucial thing to know is universals, and universals have all the characteristics that the world that's knowable has to have - they're immutable, self-consistent, motionless, non-material
Conclusion: if there's to be knowledge of reality at all, it must be of a world of universals, and not of this world.

True knowledge is knowledge of the other world - the world of universals, which includes numbers and everything else that has instances, actual or possible

The world of universals - also called the world of Essences, Forms, Ideas, or the Intelligible World

The world of particulars - or the physical world, or the sensible world - the world you grasp by the senses

Differences:

Universals are independent of the particulars in this world, and they are independent of our thoughts. Universals are actual entities, things, real, external objects, but not physical. For every class of particulars, there is a corresponding universal. Particulars are not really knowable. true knowledge is always knowledge of the forms.

Of the 2 worlds, Plato regarded the world of Forms as real.

  • Test 1: To be real, a thing has to exist. It can't be contradictory. But, to be non-contradictory, it has to be motionless, because change involves a contradiction. The things in this world are contradictory.
  • Test 2: A thing is real when it's knowable. If knowledge is of reality, and knowledge is only of the world of Forms, then the world of Forms is reality, not this world.
  • Test 3: The real is the perfect - the ideal
  • Test 4: The real is the original, as against imitations. The World of Forms is original, the res is imitation.

What is the status of this world in which we live?

It's like a byproduct, derivative, projection, or reflection of true reality. (in a fun house mirror show, you are the world of forms and the distortions in the mirrors are this world)

This world is the world of Forms reflected, or projected, into a medium

Space is the medium which enables the Forms to take on physical location.

Plato did not believe this world was created ex nihilo (out of nothing).

With one eye on the perfection of the world of Forms, he shaped and organized the matter like an architect to produce as much order, harmony, symmetry, perfection as he could.

This is the most primitive form of the Argument from Design for the existence of God.

The Demiurge left an imperfection: nothing

The Form of the Good - the ultimate Form in hte world of Forms

  • Metaphysically, it is the purpose of all existence and the source of existence.
  • Epistemologically, it is the source of all intelligibility - it's what makes anything understandable

Plato believed that the Form of the Good is ineffable. To grasp it, you must have a vision which is self-illuminating.

This is mysticism - the view that knowledge is obtainable by means other than reason or senses.

To sum up Plato's metaphysics:

There is a world of Forms, presided over by the Form of the Good, all of it reflected into space, thereby generating this half-real reflection that we call the pysical world.

Aristotle: A Philosopher for Living on Earth

Aristotle: A Philosopher for Living on Earth

Aristotelianism is philosophy as a rational science versus philosophy as a rationalization for subjective whims or mystic trances.

Aristotle (384 - 322 BC)

He studied under Plato for ~20 years. He was a Platonist.

He came to question and reject Plato's views, developing his own philosophy.

He opened The Lyceum, his own university.

"The parapatetic philosopher"

Metaphysics

  • There is one reality, this reality - the world we live in
  • Reality is objective, it's absolute, it is what it is independent of Consciousness
  • There is only this reality and no supernatural world.

He wrote polemics against the world of Forms

We can separate the common characteristics running through a group of particulars as a mental process, as a process of thought

This does not mean that this common denominator can exist in reality apart from its particular accompaniments. It is simply an abstraction - a result of selective awareness on our part.

Plato believed that because we can separate 2 things in thought, they can exist separatley in reality.

This is, says Aristotle, the fallacy of reification ("think-making"); he makes a thing out of what is simply an abstraction.

Only particular, individual, concrete things exist in reality.

Universals are distinguishable from particulars, universals are real.

Universals are real, but they exist only in particulars.

Each thing that exists is a metaphysical compound comprised of 2 elements:

  • Everything is an individual, particular concretel it is what Aristotle calls a "this"; it has something unique about it.
  • It has a certain nature; certain characteristics it shares with other things on the basis of which we can classify it; it is not only a "this", it is a "such", a certain kind of thing

Everything is a "this-such", a particular of a certain kind, an individual which belongs to a certain class.

For the universal element, he uses the word "form". For the particular, individuating element, he use the term "matter".

"Matter" stands for those aspects of a thing which make it unique; "form", those aspects of a thing it share with other things.

You never can have matter without form, nor matter without form.

Aristotle's view is called "Aristotelian realism" - Universals are real, but they only exist in particulars.

The fundamental constituent of reality is the entity - the individual thing.

Aristotle is the true author of the principle of the primacy of existence, as against the principle of the primacy of consciousness.

Primacy of existence

  • Reality is what it is independent of the content or actions of any consciousness
  • reality is the irreducible primary which sets the terms for consciousness
  • Consciousness is simply the faculty for perceiving the facts of reality

Primacy of consciousness:

  • Consciousness in some form comes first it sets the terms for reality, and reality is somehow an offshoot or derrivative or byproduct of the activities or content of consciousness.
  • facts are not what they are, but rather whatever the ruling consciousness chooses them to be

The question is "What as a matter of fact IS reality?", then "What processes of knowledge are suitable for acquiring knowledge of such a reality?".

Aristotle was the first who first discovered the primacy of reality.

He refuses to endorse any dichotomy between reality and appearance. reality is what we observe, and any theories which go counter to it are simply wrong.

Logic = "organon", metaphysics = "first philosophy"

The goal of knowledge is to understand, to find out why things happen - to see their necessity. To get to the that and the why, the facts and the causes.

What a thing bascially and essentially is determines its characteristics and its behavior. And to explain any particular thing, therefore, we must know to what class does it belong; what are its essential characteristics?

Aristotle's definition of a "definition" is "the statement of a class" - i.e., the statement of those fundamental characteristics which make the class what it is and differentiate it from all other types of thing. How do you arrive at correct definitions?

All definitions must have a certain structure; they consist of 2 parts:

  1. The general kind of thing something is - the wider, general class - known as the genus
  2. A further element specifying how the thing differs from everything else within the genus - called the differentia

If you have these 2 and you have chosen them correctly, you must have reached the essence of the class.

Rule 1: Your definition must be commensurate with the class you are defining - i.e., it must be true only of members of the class and of all of those members. You can go wrong it 2 ways on this rule: either your definition might be too wide or too narrow, take in too much territory or not enough.

Rule 2: The definition must state the fundamental characteristics of the class to be defined

We must make a firm distinction between the essence and the derivative characteristics (Aristotle called them "properties"), they are not fundamental, they are results or effects of the essence of the class.

What we do when we try to understand a fact is learn the connection between the essence of things and their properties.

Aristotle's theory of explanation: Explanation consists in seeing the particular events that we observe as instances of a general principle which relates the nature of some class to its mode of action.

You don't observe general principles; all we observe is particulars. There must be a process of acquiring the knowledge of the general principle by observing the particular facts, and this process he calls induction(epagoge) - "the process of passing in thought from particulars to a general principle"

Induction is a fundamental procedure of human knowledge, because it is the ultimate source of all of our general principles.

He had a primitive concept of induction - induction by enumeration. He had no control experimentation.

The best you could get by induction was the suggestion of alaw, which you then had to validate by some other means.

Now we can now apply the laws we've learned to new particulars.

The process of starting with the general principle and applying it to a particular case is, of course, deduction.

Induction gives you the basic general laws; deduction uses these laws to explain and understand particular instances.

Logic, Axioms & Science

What do we actually do when we defend a conclusion by stating the premises?

  • All men are mortal
  • Socrates is a man
  • Socrates is mortal

Every argument will have three terms:

  • The subject of the conclusion: the minor term
  • The predicate of the conclusion: the major term
  • The linking term, which occurs in each premise, but not in the conclusion: the middle term

This is called a syllogism

"A syllogism is a deductive argument with two premises. It contains only three terms, two of ehich are linked in the conclusion as a result of the linking of each of them with the third or middle term in the premises."

Aristotle formalized & systematized the rules of reasoning

he defined for the first time what it means to prove sometihng - to prove it or explain it - objectively.

There must be basic self-evident truths (archai). These are the foundation of human knowledge. Of these, it is improper to ask for proof because they are the ultimate foundation of everything else.

"To demand a proof of everything argues want of education" - Aristotle

Aristotle distinguishes 2 geenral types of axioms

  • Those at the base of just one science or one branch of knowledge
  • Universal axioms - axioms which you have to know to know anything (e.g. laws of logic)

Each science has special axioms unique to it. The ultimate goal is to find these first principles.

Differences from Plato's divided line:

  • The basic definitions are abstracted ultimately from sense experience and must be objectively defined; there is no mystical Goodness at the end.
  • There is no one ultimate principle from which every subject is deducible

This is how Aristotle carved out the odea of a specific science.

The laws of logic

  • The law of contradiction - nothing can be A and non-A at the same time and the same respect
  • The law of excluded middle - nothing can be A or non-A ar a given time and in a given respect
  • The law of identity - A is A

The laws of logic are laws of all reality - they are laws of being qua being

The only way we can arrive at them is by direct abstraction from self-evident sensory facts.

The faculty which grasps the self-evident is given the forbidding and misleading name "intuitive nous". Nous is the Greek word for "mind"

"Intuitive nous" simply means the human mind in its capacity to ggrasp self-evident principles, as opposed to the deductive or reasoning nous which draws conclusions from these principles

If the laws of logic are truly the foundation of all human thought, then we should be able to demonstrate that even the objector has to rely upon them - "reaffirmation through denial"

Aristotle was the first to give a formal definition of "truth" that is valid

"To say of that which is, that it is,or of that which is not that it is not, is true. To say of that which is that it is not, or of that which is not that it is, is false."

Aristotle was the first to organize and feine, in a systematic way, a great many common and widespread fallacies of reasoning.
