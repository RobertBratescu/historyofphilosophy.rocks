Introduction to philosophy

Philein (to love) + sophia (wisdom)

  1. Metaphysics - studies the nature of universe as a whole
  2. Epistemology - defines the nature and means of human knowledge
  3. *Philosophical psychology - the application of Metaphysics and Epistemology to philosopher's view on the nature of man

  1. "What is there?"(Metaphysics)
  2. "How do you know?"(Ethics)
  3. "So what?"(Ethics and Politics)

Heraclitus of Ephesus: A World of Change and Contradiction

Heraclitus (500 BC) - the first enduringly influential philosopher

"the dark" - offered oracular aphorisms without explanation

All-out attack on the law of identity

Change is incompatible with the laws of logic - change requires the existence of contradictions

Something can be A and not-A at the same time

Change necessarily requires contradictions

Everything is Change. The "world stuff" is change itself

The essence of reality is change

Everything is changing, in every respect, at every instant.

"You can't step into the same river twice, for fresh waters are always flowing in"

There are no entities

  • "Nothing is; everything is becoming"
  • "Everything flows and nothing abides"

This is a process philosophy because it holds that process, activity, motion, change, is reality.

Heraclitus' follower: Cratylus, also teacher of Plato

He stopped talking on principle, on the grounds that there is no way to give words meaning.

Parmenides of Elea: Logic Demands a Changeless Universe

Parmenides (480 BC) - Eleiaticism / Eleiatic philosophy

The first to support convictions with reasoned argument

Basic principle: What is, is, and what is not, is not, and what is not can neither be, nor be thought about

Systematic deductions

  1. The universe must be uncreated.
  2. The universe must be indestructible; it could never go out of existence. The universe is therefore eternal.
  3. Can there be such a thing as a vacuum? Absolutely not. The universe is a plenum (full).
  4. Change is impossible - change of any kind

"Father of Logic"

A seed growing into a flower. The seed is what is, the flower is what is not. The seed disappears and the flower appears. Change is a double violation of his principle: every change is a simultaneous passage from what is to what is not, ant what is not to what is. But there is no "what is not".

Agreement with Heraclitus: Change implies a contradiction - a violation of logic

Parmenides went on to deny multiplicity. It is an illusion.

Epistemologically: Rationalism

Parmenides' follower: Zeno (490 - 430 BC)

Paradoxes:

  • Motion is impossible
    It's impossible to cross a room. You have to cross half of it, and half of that, etc. You can subdivide infinitely.
    Applies to talking.
  • Multiplicity is impossible
    Multiplicity is the view that the world consists of a number of things. The world can't be a whole composed of parts.
    • If the world is a whole with parts, what size would the universe be and how many parts does it have? Infinite number of parts because every magnitude is theoretically divisible without limit and if there's an infinite number of parts we must have an infinitely big universe
    • What will be the size of the ultimate parts of the universe? There has to be such ultimate indivisible parts. But any magnitude is divisible. So the ultimate parts must be zero in size

Plato's Metaphysics: The Perceptible World and the World of Ideal Forms

Metaphysics

Agreed with Socrates - knowledge was the knowledge of universals

Premise: universals must be knowable -> then, they must exist

Where/ How do they exist? - the problem of universals

Universals and particulars have radically different characteristics -> radically different kinds of things

Two world: one with characteristics of universals, one with characteristics of particulars

Argument 1: The Argument from the Differences between Universals and Particulars

  1. There is one universal per category, but many particulars
  2. Particulars change, universals go on unchanging
  3. Particulars are primarily material; universals are abstract, nonphysical
  4. Particulars are known by means of the senses; universals are known by reason, intellect

Argument 2: The Argument of Perfection

Where do we get our concepts and standards of perfection

There is no such thing as a perfection in this world

  1. Things in this world change. If a thing changes, it couldn't be perfect, it would simply stand still, it would lack nothing
  2. Even in mathematics, the Platonists say, you'll never find perfection (perfect triangle, perfect line).
  3. We criticize things as imperfect; this presupposes that we know what perfection would consist of - and we couldn't have got it from this world

When we think of a universal, we usually have a perfect, unblemished representative in mind. The world of perfection is precisely the world of universals.

  1. Innate ideas - i.e., knowledge possessed in us at birth.
  2. The soul must be independent of the body - the groundwork to establish the immortality of the soul

Argument 3: The Argument from the Order of Knowledge

Which, in logic, would you have to learn first: universals or particulars?

Plato says it's impossible to know particulars. It is impossible to collect particular instances of justice and then abstract from them the concept of "justice". You must know beforehand what justice looks like.

There must be a realm of universals independent of this world which we knew before this life. We were born with all this knowledge, it's unconscious and we have to unearth it

Argument 4: The Argument from the Possibility of Knowledge

    You can't know a world of flux, but this world is a world of flux. If reality is to be knowable, it must be immutable - that means another world.
  1. If a world is changing, it's contradictory, and you can't know the contradictory. If a world is to be knowable, it must be made of consistent entities, entities which don't change.
  2. Knowledge acquired by the senses is invalid. Knowledges of reality must be acquired by non-sensory means; and thus it must be of a non-material object.
  3. The crucial thing to know is universals, and universals have all the characteristics that the world that's knowable has to have - they're immutable, self-consistent, motionless, non-material
Conclusion: if there's to be knowledge of reality at all, it must be of a world of universals, and not of this world.

True knowledge is knowledge of the other world - the world of universals, which includes numbers and everything else that has instances, actual or possible

The world of universals - also called the world of Essences, Forms, Ideas, or the Intelligible World

The world of particulars - or the physical world, or the sensible world - the world you grasp by the senses

Differences:

Universals are independent of the particulars in this world, and they are independent of our thoughts. Universals are actual entities, things, real, external objects, but not physical. For every class of particulars, there is a corresponding universal. Particulars are not really knowable. true knowledge is always knowledge of the forms.

Of the 2 worlds, Plato regarded the world of Forms as real.

  • Test 1: To be real, a thing has to exist. It can't be contradictory. But, to be non-contradictory, it has to be motionless, because change involves a contradiction. The things in this world are contradictory.
  • Test 2: A thing is real when it's knowable. If knowledge is of reality, and knowledge is only of the world of Forms, then the world of Forms is reality, not this world.
  • Test 3: The real is the perfect - the ideal
  • Test 4: The real is the original, as against imitations. The World of Forms is original, the res is imitation.

What is the status of this world in which we live?

It's like a byproduct, derivative, projection, or reflection of true reality. (in a fun house mirror show, you are the world of forms and the distortions in the mirrors are this world)

This world is the world of Forms reflected, or projected, into a medium

Space is the medium which enables the Forms to take on physical location.

Plato did not believe this world was created ex nihilo (out of nothing).

The myth of the Demiurge

The Demiurge is a sort of godlike, but very limited, soul that wanders freely in the universe.

With one eye on the perfection of the world of Forms, he shaped and organized the matter like an architect to produce as much order, harmony, symmetry, perfection as he could.

This is the most primitive form of the Argument from Design for the existence of God.

The Demiurge left an imperfection: nothing

The Form of the Good - the ultimate Form in the world of Forms

  • Metaphysically, it is the purpose of all existence and the source of existence.
  • Epistemologically, it is the source of all intelligibility - it's what makes anything understandable

Plato believed that the Form of the Good is ineffable. To grasp it, you must have a vision which is self-illuminating.

This is mysticism - the view that knowledge is obtainable by means other than reason or senses.

To sum up Plato's metaphysics:

There is a world of Forms, presided over by the Form of the Good, all of it reflected into space, thereby generating this half-real reflection that we call the physical world.

Aristotle: A Philosopher for Living on Earth

Aristotelianism is philosophy as a rational science versus philosophy as a rationalization for subjective whims or mystic trances.

Aristotle (384 - 322 BC)

He studied under Plato for ~20 years. He was a Platonist.

He came to question and reject Plato's views, developing his own philosophy.

He opened The Lyceum, his own university.

"The peripatetic philosopher"

Metaphysics

  • There is one reality, this reality - the world we live in
  • Reality is objective, it's absolute, it is what it is independent of Consciousness
  • There is only this reality and no supernatural world.

He wrote polemics against the world of Forms

We can separate the common characteristics running through a group of particulars as a mental process, as a process of thought

This does not mean that this common denominator can exist in reality apart from its particular accompaniments. It is simply an abstraction - a result of selective awareness on our part.

Plato believed that because we can separate 2 things in thought, they can exist separately in reality.

This is, says Aristotle, the fallacy of reification ("think-making"); he makes a thing out of what is simply an abstraction.

Only particular, individual, concrete things exist in reality.

Universals are distinguishable from particulars, universals are real.

Universals are real, but they exist only in particulars.

Each thing that exists is a metaphysical compound comprised of 2 elements:

  • Everything is an individual, particular concretely it is what Aristotle calls a "this"; it has something unique about it.
  • It has a certain nature; certain characteristics it shares with other things on the basis of which we can classify it; it is not only a "this", it is a "such", a certain kind of thing

Everything is a "this-such", a particular of a certain kind, an individual which belongs to a certain class.

For the universal element, he uses the word "form". For the particular, individuating element, he use the term "matter".

"Matter" stands for those aspects of a thing which make it unique; "form", those aspects of a thing it share with other things.

You never can have matter without form, nor matter without form.

Aristotle's view is called "Aristotelian realism" - Universals are real, but they only exist in particulars.

The fundamental constituent of reality is the entity - the individual thing.

Aristotle is the true author of the principle of the primacy of existence, as against the principle of the primacy of consciousness.

Primacy of existence

  • Reality is what it is independent of the content or actions of any consciousness
  • reality is the irreducible primary which sets the terms for consciousness
  • Consciousness is simply the faculty for perceiving the facts of reality

Primacy of consciousness:

  • Consciousness in some form comes first it sets the terms for reality, and reality is somehow an offshoot or derivative or byproduct of the activities or content of consciousness.
  • facts are not what they are, but rather whatever the ruling consciousness chooses them to be

The question is "What as a matter of fact IS reality?", then "What processes of knowledge are suitable for acquiring knowledge of such a reality?".

Aristotle was the first who first discovered the primacy of reality.

He refuses to endorse any dichotomy between reality and appearance. reality is what we observe, and any theories which go counter to it are simply wrong.

Logic = "organon", metaphysics = "first philosophy"

Aristotle’s Epistemology: Concepts, Explanation and the Nature of Science

What do we begin with in terms of knowledge? Aristotle: Nothing

We are born tabula rasa

All knowledge starts with sense experience.

This view is called empiricism - all knowledge is derived from the evidence of the sense. Since Hume, empiricism = subjectivism/skepticism (knowledge comes ONLY by the senses)

The senses are the beginning of knowledge. Concepts can be grasped by a process of abstraction from particulars without antecedent knowledge.

The process:

  1. You start with sense experience.
  2. You remember past sense experiences - i.e., you retain your percepts
  3. After a number of similar percepts are repeated, you can detect common denominators among them and abstract, and concentrate selectively on the common denominators, ignoring the differences.

On Plato's Argument of Perfection: Abstract away from the imperfection.

The goal of knowledge is to understand, to find out why things happen - to see their necessity. To get to the that and the why, the facts and the causes.

What a thing basically and essentially is determines its characteristics and its behavior. And to explain any particular thing, therefore, we must know to what class does it belong; what are its essential characteristics?

Aristotle's definition of a "definition" is "the statement of a class" - i.e., the statement of those fundamental characteristics which make the class what it is and differentiate it from all other types of thing. How do you arrive at correct definitions?

All definitions must have a certain structure; they consist of 2 parts:

  1. The general kind of thing something is - the wider, general class - known as the genus
  2. A further element specifying how the thing differs from everything else within the genus - called the differentia

If you have these 2 and you have chosen them correctly, you must have reached the essence of the class.

Rule 1: Your definition must be commensurate with the class you are defining - i.e., it must be true only of members of the class and of all of those members. You can go wrong it 2 ways on this rule: either your definition might be too wide or too narrow, take in too much territory or not enough.

Rule 2: The definition must state the fundamental characteristics of the class to be defined

We must make a firm distinction between the essence and the derivative characteristics (Aristotle called them "properties"), they are not fundamental, they are results or effects of the essence of the class.

What we do when we try to understand a fact is learn the connection between the essence of things and their properties.

Aristotle's theory of explanation: Explanation consists in seeing the particular events that we observe as instances of a general principle which relates the nature of some class to its mode of action.

You don't observe general principles; all we observe is particulars. There must be a process of acquiring the knowledge of the general principle by observing the particular facts, and this process he calls induction(epagoge) - "the process of passing in thought from particulars to a general principle"

Induction is a fundamental procedure of human knowledge, because it is the ultimate source of all of our general principles.

He had a primitive concept of induction - induction by enumeration. He had no control experimentation.

The best you could get by induction was the suggestion of a law, which you then had to validate by some other means.

Now we can now apply the laws we've learned to new particulars.

The process of starting with the general principle and applying it to a particular case is, of course, deduction.

Induction gives you the basic general laws; deduction uses these laws to explain and understand particular instances.

Aristotle: The Father of Logic

Logic, Axioms & Science

What do we actually do when we defend a conclusion by stating the premises?

  • All men are mortal
  • Socrates is a man
  • Socrates is mortal

Every argument will have three terms:

  • The subject of the conclusion: the minor term
  • The predicate of the conclusion: the major term
  • The linking term, which occurs in each premise, but not in the conclusion: the middle term

This is called a syllogism

"A syllogism is a deductive argument with two premises. It contains only three terms, two of which are linked in the conclusion as a result of the linking of each of them with the third or middle term in the premises."

Aristotle formalized & systematized the rules of reasoning

he defined for the first time what it means to prove something - to prove it or explain it - objectively.

There must be basic self-evident truths (archai). These are the foundation of human knowledge. Of these, it is improper to ask for proof because they are the ultimate foundation of everything else.

"To demand a proof of everything argues want of education" - Aristotle

Aristotle distinguishes 2 general types of axioms

  • Those at the base of just one science or one branch of knowledge
  • Universal axioms - axioms which you have to know to know anything (e.g. laws of logic)

Each science has special axioms unique to it. The ultimate goal is to find these first principles.

Differences from Plato's divided line:

  • The basic definitions are abstracted ultimately from sense experience and must be objectively defined; there is no mystical Goodness at the end.
  • There is no one ultimate principle from which every subject is deducible

This is how Aristotle carved out the idea of a specific science.

The laws of logic

  • The law of contradiction - nothing can be A and non-A at the same time and the same respect
  • The law of excluded middle - nothing can be A or non-A ar a given time and in a given respect
  • The law of identity - A is A

The laws of logic are laws of all reality - they are laws of being qua being

The only way we can arrive at them is by direct abstraction from self-evident sensory facts.

The faculty which grasps the self-evident is given the forbidding and misleading name "intuitive nous". Nous is the Greek word for "mind"

"Intuitive nous" simply means the human mind in its capacity to grasp self-evident principles, as opposed to the deductive or reasoning nous which draws conclusions from these principles

If the laws of logic are truly the foundation of all human thought, then we should be able to demonstrate that even the objector has to rely upon them - "reaffirmation through denial"

Aristotle was the first to give a formal definition of "truth" that is valid

"To say of that which is, that it is, or of that which is not that it is not, is true. To say of that which is that it is not, or of that which is not that it is, is false."

Aristotle was the first to organize and fine, in a systematic way, a great many common and widespread fallacies of reasoning.

Aristotle’s Metaphysics: Matter, Form and Causality

Metaphysics: Universals and Particulars

There are 2 elements comprising each thing - a particularizing or individuating element, that which makes it a "this" (Aristotle called this "matter"), and the common or universalizing element (which he called "form") - two aspects which are separable in thought but not in reality.

In general, everything is made of some stuff, and that material is organized, structured, formed in some particular way.

The form (now synonymous with the structure) is what gives the thing its character; the material or stuff is what's unique to it, what makes it this particular instance of its class.

What's universal comes from structure; particularity from stuff, and so form and matter come now to mean structure-stuff

To this day, Aristotelian logic is called formal logic because Aristotle's discovery was that the validity of your reasoning depends exclusively upon the form, upon the structure.

Matter:

The problem of Change - solved by Aristotle

It's the same and it's not the same, but in 2 different ways. The matter has taken the new form; the matter is now different in respect of its organization

There's no change - the material has always existed, it merely changed it form. Change does not involve a contradiction.

Change is the process of matter taking on new form. We can start to speak of a thing as being matter relative to a later state of development

Bricks, we can say, are matter for a house, but it's clearer to say bricks are potentially a house. And the house, when it comes, is the actuality of the bricks, the actualization of their potentiality - the thing which contains, in full reality, what had earlier existed only potentially.

Matter is any material which has potentialities for reorganization, any material which can become or do something. And form is any structure in which these potentialities are actualized.

Any matter can be the actuality for previous matter and the potentiality of future actualities. (Sense data -> Concept)

It is the metaphysical foundation for the view that reality is lawful and orderly, because what a thing is matter for depends on its nature, on what it is actually.

It is not true that anything is possible.

It is not true that anything could happen.

What a thing can do (its potentialities) depends on and derives from what it is (its actualities).

Actualities determine potentialities - a fundamental law of Aristotelian metaphysics.

This is the basis for a formal proof of the law of cause and effect.

Combined with the law of identity: A thing in a given set of circumstances can act only in one way, the way dictated by its nature - in other words, same cause, same effect.

  • The statue at the end - the formal cause

  • The sculptor's purpose, end or goal - the final cause

  1. Jesus was Jewish; there is therefore a large influence of Judaism on Christianity
  2. As the Jesus movement became a separate mystery cult, taking Jesus as its god, it acquired further mystery cult trappings and dogmas that were not possessed by Judaism.
  3. As its spokesmen tried to make it intellectually a respectable, they borrowed from Greek philosophy. And in so doing, they borrowed profoundly from Plato and above all Plotinus

Saint Augustine on Knowledge and Reality

Augustine (354 - 430 AD)

First real philosopher of Christianity.

He follows Plato. We cannot have knowledge of this world.

He followed Plotinus and thought of the Forms as thoughts in the mind of God. True knowledge consists of turning away from this world and discovering the Forms in God's mind.

God himself must communicate the truths to us. man is dependent on God for all knowledge; he's dependent on a special act of divine "illumination”.

Augustine makes man much more helpless, much more dependent on the supernatural.

Augustine's main point in regard to faith is that no man can set his own reason up as judge of the truth or falsehood of Christian teachings.

To do so would be to rely on one's puny intellect and commit the sin of intellectual pride.

That which God has revealed to man must be accepted on faith - it must be assented to because it comes from God, even if you don't understand why it's true

"one must first believe in order that one may then know."

Augustine's metaphysics

The central concept is Augustine's concept of "God"

God is, in part, an infinite mind containing the Platonic Forms - to that extent, He's like Plotinus' divine mind.

God is the only fully real entity; He is immutable, perfect, etc. - just like Plato's other world, or the Form of the Good.

Augustine describes Him as a living, knowing, willing being, all-powerful, all-knowing, all-good, the Father of His children Who loves them, Who protects them, Who judges them, Who sends His son down to help them, etc.

Christianity also had the problem of how to conceive the trinity

Christianity construes Jesus as a divine being - the son of God, and therefore a god himself, so there are 2 gods, yet Christianity is monotheistic.

There is a trinity, each are God, and yet there is only one God. The ultimate position, adopted formally by the Church, was that this is a mystery which surpasses human understanding and must be accepted on faith.

Augustine has to try to make some kind of coherence out of a being who has to combine:

  • the emotionless neo-platonic divine mind
  • the primitive Judaic loving, personal Father
  • the Christian trinity which is and isn't three

God, in Augustine's view, created this world ex nihilo (out of nothing) by an act of will, at a particular point in time - a profound contrast with any Greek view.

All the Greeks, Plotinus included, believed that the universe was eternal, that its constituent elements had always existed.

God runs every aspect of what occurs in the universe as part of His plan. According to Augustine, God alone has causal efficacy in the universe. Everything is determined by God.

On the Christian view, life on earth is like a play by Shakespeare, and it becomes meaningful to ask, "What does it mean?", "What did the Author have in mind?"

The fact of existence is not a primary, but a mystery which requires unravelling. Everything is a symbol pointing beyond itself to God.

"Why did God include so much evil, so much suffering, in His play?"

The Manichee’s (a sect to which Augustine belonged to as a youth) thought the only solution was to say that God is limited, that there is a Devil and he is responsible for evil. Christianity repudiated this as heretical.

Why does God allow evil? If you saw God's purpose, you would see that everything is really good.

Augustine takes the Neo-platonic view that evil is the absence if the perfection and reality that is inherent in the creature being non-God

All things in this world are necessarily infected with a metaphysical deficiency.

It follows that all men, insofar as they're semi-real members of this semi-real world, are also inherently infected with evil.

Pelagius held that each human should enters the world sinless, that it has free will, is capable on its own of shaping its destiny by its own choices, and therefore is responsible for its own actions, and properly by God. He denied any intrinsic wickedness in man.

Free will is incompatible with man's inherent evil, and with God's rigid predestination, with the doctrine that only God has causal efficacy.

Christianity is deterministic on many counts; it is incapable of accepting determinism consistently.

There is no possible solution on his premises, and every solution Augustine gives he contradicts elsewhere.

On the issue of evil and freedom, therefore, the upshot is a mass of contradictions in Augustine and in Christianity

  • God is the cause of everything; and yet, He is not responsible for evil
  • This world and man are inherently evil; and yet man is responsible for his sins and properly to be condemned by God for them
  • Man is helpless to be virtuous on his own, yet he is to bear responsibility for vices he cannot alter.

Augustine agrees with Plato and Plotinus that this world is not fully real. It is kind of shadowy reflection of true reality - i.e., of the realm of God.

Saint Augustine and the Ethics of Christianity

Augustine's ethics

The essence is not the matter of right living but of right loving. The essence of the good life is not in what you accomplish or create during this life, but in giving the proper inner emotional allegiance to the true dimension, to God. This is the primacy of motive over action that we saw in discussing the Stoics - the primacy of the inner over the outer

It can be called the primacy of the consciousness over existence. In this sense it's the soul-body dichotomy present in Greek thought since the Pythagoreans and Orphics.

Man is a temporary union of 2 distinct substances: the soul and the body.

All that counts is escape - i.e., reunion with God in Heaven.

If all men are corrupt, how does God decide whom to select for Heaven? It is inexplicable to man.

The utter dependence of man on God

  • In epistemology, man depends on God's illumination for knowledge.
  • In ethics, he depends on God's grace for virtue
  • In metaphysics, man is a semi-real creature, created, run and ruled by God.

Why have ethics? You can't achieve ethics on earth, you can't earn entry into heaven. That is a corrupt question. God has commanded that you act in certain ways, and these commandments must be obeyed because God commanded them. The highest virtue is obedience to God, doing your duty because it is your duty.

Christianity is definitely inconsistent on this point. There are several anti-duty, pro-egoist elements:

  • There is an emphasis on the salvation of your own individual soul.
  • You will be rewarded with eternal happiness of you make it to heaven - and it's your own eternal happiness that you will experience.
  • "Love thy neighbor as thyself" implies that you can love yourself to some extent.

All of these elements of egoism are legacies of the Greek influence on Christianity.

What is it that God commands?

What He wants primarily is that man should turn away from life on earth and turn his attention to God.

"He that loveth his life shall lose it; he that hateth it shall gain it unto ife eternal"

By the time of Christianity, suffering as such - that is, on earth - as become a value.

"The problem of pleasure" - how to sustain yourself in the most frugal way without enjoying it.

What should you attitude be to yourself? You should recognize the facts: you're stained with sin, you're worthless, and you should estimate yourself accordingly.

Consequently, the great virtue is humility; the great vice is pride

Science and learning represent lusts also this-worldly loves and concerns which are futile and to be suspended

It's arrogant of puny man to pursue science on his own - what God wants man to know, God will reveal in his own good time.

The answer to everything is: "That is how it is because God wills it"

Your distinctive virtue, qua Christian, is to love men who do not meet your standards, who do not give you pleasure, to love men who are out to harm you - "Love your enemies, bless them that curse you, pray for them that despitefully use and persecute you"

The Dark Ages, Medieval Scholasticism and the Rediscovery of Aristotle

"Scholasticism" is a general name for the philosophy of the whole Middle Age period, especially from the last half of the 11th - 14th centuries

Philosophy, for them, was an attempt to substantiate rationally and to harmonize the dogmas of the appropriate authorities.

No comprehensive philosophic systems.

Are universals real or not?

  • Platonism - universals are real, separate from particulars. Heavily criticized on the theological grounds.
  • Nominalism - only particulars exist, each is through and through unique. Universals are names that people attribute to groups of particulars. This makes all conceptual thought completely arbitrary and detached from reality.
  • Middle position - "moderate realism". Only particulars exist, but human beings, by abstraction, are able to discover a common nature in a number of particulars. The universal, white it exists in one sense only in the mind - as an abstraction - is not subjective fiction because individual things do have common properties which form an objective basis for out abstractions

To some, reason was a dangerous enemy of Christianity and the whole attempt of the Scholastic to make sense of the dogmas was depraved (Bernard of Clairvaux)

You start with faith. Faith is the basis of reason, and maybe there'll be bits of the faith or large hunks that you can't explain, but that's simply temporary so you couldn't possibly criticize faith.

Anselm worked out an argument for the existence of God which became very famous - it's called the "ontological argument":

  • From the very term "God" you can prove that He exists. Simply granting that you have an idea of God - and everybody has an idea of God. God is "the being than which nothing greater can be conceived". Between 2 identical gods, the one who exists is better. "I have a concept of an absolutely perfect being, but he doesn't exist," you're saying: "I have a concept of an absolutely perfect Being who lacks a perfection," which is a contradiction. Logic itself demands the existence of God.

Thomas Aquinas: The Union of Aristotelianism and Christianity

Thomas Aquinas (1225 - 1275)

He has a monumental, ingenious philosophic system, more through, more systematic, than any in all of philosophy prior to his time. It is an attempted synthesis of Aristotle and Christianity.

Epistemology

The main problem was to reconcile reason and faith. Aquinas did so by distinguishing between philosophy and theology.

Philosophy begins with the evidence of the senses and proceeds strictly by reason and logic. It's a completely natural subject.

Theology begins with the dogmas of faith, with the revelations, and then tries to explore and explicate their full meaning

Philosophy is primarily concerned with the facts of this world. Theology is primarily concerned with the mysteries of religion.

  • Natural knowledge
  • revealed knowledge
  • middle - "natural theology"

Revealed theology: what is its relation to reason and philosophy?

Answer: revelation can in no way contradict reason; it can maintain nothing that can be rationally refuted

This view was a charter of liberty and liberation for human reason. Reason requires no special divine aid.

For Augustine, faith is the basis of reason

For Tertullian, faith contradicts reason

For Aquinas, faith is not the basis or antagonist of reason, merely a supplement to it.

Metaphysics

  • A supernatural God is the creator and source of this world
  • God's mind contains the Platonic Forms
  • The relative unreality of this world compared to the true reality of God
  • All the Catholic dogmas: the trinity, the incarnation, etc.

Aristotelian Metaphysics

The central problem confronting Aquinas in metaphysics is how to reconcile God and this world.

He took over from Aristotle the view that there is only one reality, but that it is hierarchical in nature - there are various levels of reality depending upon the extent to which form is actualized.

Nature is merely a lower level of the one continuous hierarchy.

On this view of Aquinas', science is metaphysically possible again.

How is science possible? For Aquinas, God created the world ex nihilo - everything happens as He ordains by His will. An orderly, lawful, regular universe where entities act in accord with their natures is part of what God wills - a scientifically understandable universe is part of His plan.

So ultimately only God has causal efficacy, but He expresses it by endowing His creatures with causal efficacy.

God can intervene directly at any time - that will be a miracle.

Aquinas' arguments for the existence of God

There are 5 famous arguments - none of them original with him

  1. The argument from motion - it literally restates Aristotle's argument for the Prime Mover, arguing that motion implies a first mover
  2. The argument from efficient causation -
    • Everything has an efficient cause which brought it into existence
    • Nothing can be the efficient cause of itself
    • So everything is caused by something previous
    • But we can't have an infinite regress
    • He concludes: there must be an uncaused cause, a first cause
  3. The argument from necessity and contingency
    • Some entities are contingent - it's possible for them not to exist
    • It is not possible that all entities could be contingent (e.g., Existence)
    • Therefore, something must be necessary
    • If so, either it's necessary by its own nature, or it owes its necessity to something else
    • But we can't have an infinite regress
    • And this is what we mean by God - the absolutely necessary being
  4. The argument from degrees
    • There are degrees of various qualities that we observe
    • Where there are degrees of quality, there must be an absolute maximum of the quality somewhere, which is the cause of its existence in all the lesser degrees
    • There are degrees of goodness
    • There must therefore be a maximum of goodness which causes all the lesser degrees of goodness, absolute goodness or perfection
    • And this is God
  5. The teleological argument
    • There is order in the world
    • Order implies final causation, teleology
    • And teleology implies a conscious being who is imposing the purpose or end or goal on things
    • So there must be such a cosmic consciousness, namely God

Now he has to show that the God he has proved is the God of Christianity. But how do you get there?

Ethics

Here again he attempted to reconcile Aristotle with Christianity

  • Natural virtues
  • Teleological virtues

The natural virtues are Aristotelian (self-realization, the all-round development of one's capacities, living by reason, satisfying your this worldly desires to a moderate extent, happiness on earth)

This side is discoverable by reason alone, and to some degree man can achieve the natural virtues by his own efforts

Beside the natural, rational virtues, there are the theological virtues: faith, hope, charity. They cannot be proved by reason. they depend on God's revelation for us to know that they're virtues. And they cannot be attained by our own efforts, only by God's grace.

he accepted the proclaimed official Church dogma.

Politics

Just as grace perfects nature, so the Church perfects the State; and just as God is supreme over nature, therefore the Church must be supreme over the State.

The Separation of Faith and Reason: The Transition from the Middle Ages to the Renaissance

During the Renaissance, men typically did not dispute the existence of God or the veracity of revelations or original sin or the incarnation or the whole slew of dogmas. They simply didn't focus on them anymore in anything like the manner that they had prior to the Renaissance.

As a fundamental, controlling, exclusive concern in human life, religious supernaturalism died in the Renaissance, never to recover.

After Aquinas, philosophy and theology fell apart altogether

When this occurred, philosophy lost all essential connection with theology, and the Scholastic enterprise collapsed.

Theological issues were no longer of relevance to the philosopher; they were matters of faith.

"Entities are not to be multiplied beyond necessity" - Occam's Razor - it slashes the occult

Faith and reason are inherently in conflict: there is the truth in theology and the truth in philosophy
